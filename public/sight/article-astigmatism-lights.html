The Science of Astigmatism: From Blurry Lights to HD Vision

Technical Guide by VisionGuard Optics

January 20, 2026 • 11 minute read

Have you ever driven at night and noticed that street lamps and car headlights appear to have long, streaky rays of light coming off them? This phenomenon, often referred to as astigmatism lights, is a classic symptom of a common refractive error. While many people think they just have "blurry eyes," a professional astigmatism test often reveals that the shape of their eye is preventing light from focusing properly.

Astigmatism vs Normal: What Causes Astigmatism?

In a perfect world, our eyes would be shaped like perfectly round basketballs. This is what we call "normal" vision. However, for many, the cornea or lens is curved more in one direction than the other—shaped more like a football. When comparing astigmatism vs normal, the key difference is how light behaves as it enters the eye. In a normal eye, light converges onto a single point on the retina. In an eye with astigmatism, light is focused on multiple points, leading to distortion.

But what causes astigmatism in the first place? Most cases are hereditary; you are simply born with an eye shape that isn't perfectly spherical. Other times, it can develop after an eye injury or surgery. It is a common misconception that reading in low light or sitting too close to the TV causes it. While those habits cause eye strain, they do not change the physical curvature of your cornea.

Stigmatism vs Astigmatism: Clearing the Confusion

Oftentimes, you'll hear people use the term stigmatism. In reality, stigmatism vs astigmatism isn't a comparison between two different conditions. "Stigmatism" is usually just a mispronunciation of "astigmatism." In medical terms, "stigma" means a point; "a-stigmatism" literally means "without a point," referring to the lack of a single focal point for light. If you tell your doctor you have stigmatism, they will know you mean astigmatism, but using the correct term helps you better understand the science behind your vision.

Corrective Options: Astigmatism Glasses and Contacts

The good news is that correcting this condition is easier than ever. Most people start with astigmatism glasses. These lenses have a special "cylindrical" power that compensates for the uneven curvature of your eye. When shopping for astigmatism glasses, ensure your prescription includes the "Cylinder" and "Axis" values, as these are the precise measurements of your eye's distortion.

For those who prefer a frame-free look, contact lenses for astigmatism—also known as toric lenses—are highly effective. Unlike regular contacts, contact lenses for astigmatism are weighted at the bottom to ensure they stay in a specific orientation on your eye. If a toric lens rotates, your vision will become blurry again. This is why a proper fitting by an eye care professional is essential.

Interestingly, you can even find colored contacts for astigmatism. In the past, people with astigmatism were limited to clear lenses, but modern manufacturing now allows for vibrant colored contacts for astigmatism that provide both clear vision and a new look.

Beyond Lenses: Can LASIK Fix Astigmatism?

A frequently asked question for those tired of maintaining lenses is: can lasik fix astigmatism? The answer is a resounding yes. LASIK (Laser-Assisted in Situ Keratomileusis) uses a cold laser to reshape the cornea. By removing microscopic amounts of tissue, the surgeon can make the cornea more spherical, effectively "curing" the astigmatism for most patients. While not everyone is a candidate, can lasik fix astigmatism is usually the first question discussed during a refractive surgery consultation.

Take an Astigmatism Test

If you aren't sure if you have it, you can look for an astigmatism test online or via mobile apps. A common test involves a series of radial lines in a circle. If some lines look darker or thicker than others while you are at a distance, it's a strong indicator that you should visit a professional for a comprehensive astigmatism test.
